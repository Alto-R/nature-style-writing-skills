# 01 — Title

## Role

The title states the single most important finding as a claim, in the fewest words that
still locate the discovery. It foregrounds *the result*, not the method or the data.

## Structure

A short declarative noun phrase, **~8–12 words**, usually:

> **A [scope] [finding-noun] in [domain/system]**

- **[scope]** — the reach of the claim: *global, widespread, pervasive, persistent*, or a
  quantified qualifier. Optional but common.
- **[finding-noun]** — the thing you discovered: *gap, trade-off, limit, mismatch,
  divergence, threshold, pattern, decline, constraint*. This is the heart of the title.
- **[domain/system]** — where it holds: the study system, generalised.

It can also be a plain claim: **"[Subject] [verb a finding] across [population]"**.

## Patterns

- Declarative, not a question, not a methods description.
- Foreground the *tension or result* (a gap, a limit, an asymmetry), not the tool.
- No hedging words in the title (no *may, could, suggests*) — the title asserts.
- No abbreviations or acronyms unless universally known.
- Sentence case; no terminal punctuation.

## Templates (placeholders only)

- "A global [finding-noun] in [system] [process]"
- "[Driver] limits [outcome] across [population]"
- "[Outcome] is constrained by [mechanism], not [the obvious alternative]"

## Do / Don't

- ❌ "A novel framework for assessing [X]" — method-led and uses *novel*.
- ❌ "Can [X] achieve [Y]? A global assessment" — question + method.
- ✅ "A global [finding-noun] in [domain]" — result-led, locates the claim.

## Self-check

Is the title a claim about a *finding* (not the method)? ≤ ~12 words? No novelty words, no
hedging, no undefined acronyms, no end punctuation, sentence case?
