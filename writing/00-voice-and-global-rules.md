# 00 — Voice and global rules

These apply to **every** section. Read once before drafting anything; the section files
assume them.

## The voice (how the paper sounds)

A Nature-branded research paper reads like a confident, precise, slightly austere expert
who never oversells. Five habits produce it:

1. **Lead with the finding, not the method.** State *what is true* first; the apparatus
   that established it comes second or in Methods. ("X remains out of reach for many
   [units]" before "using a Y assessment of Z").
2. **Quantify everything.** Replace adjectives with numbers and units. Not "costs vary
   widely" but "[mean] ± [SD], median [value], ranging [a–b]". Give a distribution (mean,
   median, spread), not just a central value.
3. **Compare explicitly.** A number alone is inert; anchor it. "[value], about [k] times
   that of [reference group]"; "[x]% of the sample".
4. **Hedge what is inferred, stay decisive on the headline.** Use *indicate, suggest, is
   associated with, likely* for mechanisms and projections; state the central result
   plainly. Hedging is for inference, not for the main claim.
5. **Topic sentence first.** Each paragraph opens with the sentence that states its point;
   the rest supplies evidence and mechanism.

## Sentence & paragraph organisation

- One claim per paragraph, announced in the first sentence.
- Weave mechanism into description — don't separate "here is the pattern" from "here is
  why"; e.g. "[group] show [magnitude] because [driver]".
- Connectives carry the logic: *Therefore, As a result, This suggests* (causal); *In
  addition, Furthermore* (additive); *By contrast, However, Although, Despite* (contrast).

## Global rules baked into all prose

These are editorial requirements, stated where you will break them most often.

- **No novelty or exaggeration words.** Do **not** write *novel, new, first, unprecedented,
  remarkable, outstanding, revolutionary, exceptional, extraordinary, extremely*. Let the
  result carry the weight. Replace "we present a novel framework" → "we develop a
  framework".
- **Soften absolute claims.** Avoid bare *without / does not / cannot / proves* in central
  findings. Prefer *alone, unlikely, insufficient, may, suggesting, in the absence of
  additional [support]*. Example: "cost declines will not deliver equitable outcomes" →
  "cost declines **alone are unlikely to** deliver equitable outcomes".
- **Use "we" sparingly** — roughly **< 1 per 1,000 words** of body text. Acceptable: "Here
  we show…" (abstract), "we develop…" (the study statement), and Methods steps. Results and
  Discussion should be largely objective/passive ("the analysis reveals", "these results
  indicate"), **not** "we find / we see / we observe" repeated. Rewrite "We find that X
  rises" → "X rises".
- **No emphasis formatting in running text.** No italics, **bold**, underline, or
  "quotation marks" for emphasis. Italics are allowed only for: scalar mathematical
  variables (see `04`), journal/book titles in references, and conventional data-citation
  quoting of a dataset title.
- **Define each abbreviation once, at first use in the running text**, then use the short
  form. Do not redefine it later in the body. (Captions and tables are the exception — they
  must be self-contained and re-define their own acronyms; see `07`.)
- **No bullet or numbered lists** anywhere in the main text or Supplementary Information.
  Express enumerations as prose: "Three factors contribute. First, … Second, … Third, …".
- **No mid-sentence colon to introduce a finding.** Write a complete sentence instead.
  ("The result is clear: costs rise." → "Costs rise across the sample.") Colons are fine in
  headings, figure-caption significance keys, and reference subtitles.

## Headings

- **One level of subheadings only** inside Results and Methods (no nested sub-sub-headings).
- **Sentence case** (capitalise only the first word and proper nouns), applied consistently.
- **No terminal punctuation** on headings (no full stop, colon, or em-dash). Parentheses to
  expand an abbreviation are acceptable.

## Tense & voice cheat-sheet

| Where | Tense / voice |
|---|---|
| General truths, the gap, the main claim | Present |
| What the study does ("Here we show / we develop") | Present |
| Specific actions performed ("we analysed", "profiles were simulated") | Past, often passive |
| How a method/system works or what a constraint enforces | Present passive |
| Results patterns and their interpretation | Present |

## Self-check

Sparse "we"? No novelty/exaggeration words? Absolute claims softened? No emphasis
formatting or bullets? Each abbreviation defined once? Headings one level, sentence case,
no end punctuation? Every number carries a unit and a comparison?
