# 03 — Introduction

## Role

Move the reader from broad significance to the precise gap your study fills, then state
what the study does. It funnels: wide → narrow → gap → "Here we".

## Structure (paragraph by paragraph, ~5 paragraphs)

1. **Broad significance.** Open with why this problem matters at the largest relevant scale
   (societal goal, scientific stakes, who is affected). 2–4 sentences.
2. **Why this class of problem is distinct.** Explain what makes the studied system
   different from the obvious comparators, and enumerate the structural features that create
   a distinct context. End by tying them together: "Together, these characteristics create a
   distinct [techno-economic / ecological / clinical] context…".
3. **State of knowledge and the real constraint.** Acknowledge recent progress, then locate
   the *actual* barrier — often not the obvious one. Pattern: "Despite [progress], the
   fundamental barrier lies not in [the assumed cause] but in [the real cause]." Name the
   core tension that your finding will be about.
4. **Prior-work limitation = the gap.** Summarise what previous studies did and why it is
   insufficient (single-case, partial view, one factor in isolation, no joint treatment).
   State the gap as a *structural* shortfall, then its consequence. Pattern: "Previous
   studies have largely focused on [narrow scope], providing only a partial view of
   [phenomenon]… it remains unclear [the open question]. This knowledge gap limits both
   [understanding] and [practical decision]."
5. **"Here we…" study statement.** What you do, the scale, and the analytical dimensions.
   Pattern: "To address this gap, we develop [a framework/assessment] that evaluates
   [phenomenon] as [the key relationship]. Drawing on [N units] and [M data points], we
   examine [dimension 1], [dimension 2], and [dimension 3]." This paragraph is **present
   tense** and **introduces the present study**.

## Tense & voice

- Paragraphs 1–4: present tense for general truths; past for specific prior findings.
- Paragraph 5: **present tense**, the only place "we develop / we examine" appears.

## Rules baked in

- **Final paragraph is present tense and introduces the study** (a Nature structural rule).
- Define every abbreviation at first use here if it first appears in the Introduction
  (per `00`).
- Soften forward-looking phrasing; no novelty/exaggeration words (per `00`).
- Citations are numerical superscripts in citation order (see `08`).

## Do / Don't

- ❌ A literature review for its own sake. Every cited limitation must point at *your* gap.
- ❌ Ending the Introduction with results. End with what the study *does*, not what it found.
- ✅ A funnel where paragraph 4 names the gap and paragraph 5 fills it.

## Self-check

Does it funnel wide→narrow→gap→study? Is the real constraint named (not just the obvious
one)? Does the gap sentence say what "remains unclear"? Is the last paragraph present tense,
introducing the study with its scale and dimensions?
